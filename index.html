The Foundation

What is a DCF and why does it have 17 types?

A Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model values an asset as the present value of all future cash flows it will generate, discounted at a rate that reflects their risk. The core idea is simple: a pound received in the future is worth less than a pound today.

The standard formula is clean: Enterprise Value = the sum of all future free cash flows divided by (1 + WACC) to the power of t, plus a terminal value. But this clean formula breaks in different ways for different types of businesses — and that is why 17 variants exist.

A bank cannot use FCFF because its debt is its product, not a financing choice. An airport concession has no terminal value because the asset returns to the government at concession end. A pharmaceutical pipeline requires probability-weighted cash flows because clinical trials have binary outcomes. A mining company needs a finite reserve-depletion model, not a perpetuity.

Core Formula
EV = Σ [ FCFFt / (1+WACC)^t ]
+ Terminal Value / (1+WACC)^n
Standard DCF works when…
The business generates stable, predictable free cash flows. Revenue grows at a consistent rate. Capital structure is simple. Think: Infosys, Unilever, Honeywell.
Standard DCF breaks when…
You apply Gordon Growth terminal value to a finite asset (mine, concession). You use FCFF for a bank. You ignore patent cliffs for a drug company. You treat a marketplace's gross revenue as net revenue. Each failure mode has its own correct model.
The key skill
Selecting the right model for the business in front of you — and knowing exactly why the alternatives would be wrong. That judgment is what separates a technically strong analyst from a very fast spreadsheet user.

Deep Dives

Four Concepts That Separate Good Analysts

Concept 01
Terminal Value — and when it doesn't exist

Terminal value typically accounts for 60–80% of a DCF's total enterprise value. It is calculated using the Gordon Growth model: TV = FCFFn+1 / (WACC – g), where g is the long-run growth rate. It assumes the business continues growing at rate g forever.

This assumption is fine for Coca-Cola. It is catastrophically wrong for an airport concession (asset returns to government in 2036), a mining operation (ore body depletes in 20 years), or a pharmaceutical patent (expires and revenue drops 85% to generics).

When g approaches WACC, terminal value approaches infinity. This is the most dangerous moment in a DCF — a small change in g near WACC swings enterprise value by billions. Always run a sensitivity table.
1
Perpetuity Growth (Gordon Growth)
TV = FCFFn+1 / (WACC – g). Used for mature, stable businesses. g should anchor to nominal GDP growth (2–4%). Never set g above WACC.
2
Exit Multiple Method
TV = Exit EBITDA × Peer EV/EBITDA multiple. Useful cross-check. Circularity risk: the multiple embeds other analysts' growth assumptions.
3
No Terminal Value
Concessions (GMR, toll roads), mines, pharma patents. The model runs only for the asset's useful life. Every analyst who applies Gordon Growth to an airport concession makes a fundamental error.
4
Cap Rate Exit (Real Estate)
TV = NOI_exit / Cap Rate. The cap rate is real estate's equivalent of WACC–g. Prologis exits at 4.5%, Embassy REIT at 8%.
1
Risk-Free Rate
10-year government bond yield in the currency of cash flows. India INR DCF: 7.1% (10Y G-Sec). US USD: 4.5% (10Y UST). Never mix currency — rupee cash flows need a rupee discount rate.
2
Equity Risk Premium (ERP)
The extra return investors demand for equity over risk-free. Source: Damodaran's annual country ERP database. US: 5.5%. India: ~7%. Always cite your source.
3
Beta (β)
Measures sensitivity to market moves. 5-year monthly regression vs benchmark. β=1 = moves with the market. Coca-Cola β=0.60 (defensive). Tesla β=1.8 (amplifies market). Re-lever for target capital structure.
4
Cost of Debt × (1–tax)
Interest expense / average debt, then tax-shield it. Use market yield, not coupon. Weight equity and debt by market value — never book value.
Concept 02
Building WACC from First Principles

WACC = Ke × We + Kd(1–t) × Wd. Every element is a deliberate choice. The most common errors: using book value weights instead of market value weights, using the wrong risk-free rate for the currency, and applying one WACC across completely different business segments.

For a conglomerate like Tata Group, using one WACC for both TCS (IT services, bond-proxy, β=1.0) and Tata Steel (commodity, cyclical, β=1.4) would simultaneously undervalue TCS and understate steel risk. SOTP exists precisely because of this.

WACC is a rate for discounting the enterprise. DDM uses Cost of Equity (Ke) only — dividends flow to equity holders, not the firm. This is one of the most commonly tested interview distinctions.
Concept 03
Holding Company Discount in Indian SOTP

When a parent company holds stakes in several listed subsidiaries, the parent's market cap is almost always less than the sum of those stakes. This gap is the holding company discount — typically 15–30% for Indian conglomerates.

The discount exists for real reasons: the parent adds complexity and opacity, profitable subsidiaries may cross-subsidise loss-making ones, and investors can simply buy the subsidiaries directly without the intermediary. For Tata Sons (unlisted), TCS alone represents approximately 60% of gross NAV — meaning the entire group's valuation is fundamentally a leveraged bet on Indian IT services.

Reliance trades at ~8% HoldCo discount (transparent reporting). Bajaj Holdings trades at ~38% discount (pure investment holding company). Knowing why the range varies this widely is the interview test.
Factors that widen the discount
Opacity and poor reporting. Cross-subsidisation of loss-making units. Complex governance. Lack of transparency on unlisted subsidiaries. Conglomerate discount for unrelated diversification.
Factors that narrow the discount
Good governance and clear segment reporting. Unlisted subsidiary optionality not priced in listed stocks. Brand value accruing to the holdco. Strong promoter track record of value creation.
India benchmark range
Reliance 8% · Tata Group 20% · Mahindra 15% · ITC 20% · Bajaj Holdings 38%. The Tata Trust governance model (long-term, non-profit) actually justifies a narrower discount than most — one of the few positive governance arguments.
1
Phase Success Rates
Ph1→Ph2: 65%. Ph2→Ph3: 40%. Ph3→Approval: 60%. Approval→Launch: 90%. Cumulative: ~14%. Memorise these. Interviewers test them directly. Diabetes/obesity runs higher (~23%), oncology runs lower (~5%).
2
Probability-Weight Each Drug
rNPV per drug = PoS × Peak NOPAT annuity × discount factor for time to launch. A Phase 3 drug at 60% PoS contributes 60% of its expected peak sales NPV. A Phase 2 drug at 28% contributes far less — but if its data is strong, it's a real option.
3
Model the Patent Cliff Explicitly
Marketed drugs lose exclusivity at patent expiry. Revenue typically falls 80–90% within 2 years of generic entry. Gordon Growth would treat Ozempic as a perpetuity. The correct model cuts revenue ~85% in FY2032.
4
SOTP: Marketed + Pipeline
Total pharma value = Marketed drugs standard DCF + Pipeline rNPV sum + Net cash. Novo Nordisk: marketed franchise contributes ~DKK 600Bn, pipeline rNPV ~DKK 300Bn. The split determines whether the stock is cheap or expensive.
Concept 04
Risk-Adjusted NPV for Pharmaceutical Pipelines

Pharma companies hold two types of assets: marketed drugs with known revenues, and pipeline drugs with binary, uncertain outcomes. Standard DCF handles the first. rNPV handles the second by probability-weighting each drug's future cash flows by the likelihood it actually reaches market.

The result is a more honest answer than either pure optimism (every drug succeeds) or pure pessimism (only proved revenue counts). The probability weights come from decades of clinical trial data — and they are specific to therapeutic area, not universal.

The analogy to other models: rNPV is to pharma what Monte Carlo is to high-uncertainty businesses. Both replace a false point estimate with an honest probability-weighted value. The key difference: pharma PoS rates are empirically grounded, while Monte Carlo distributions are assumed.

Interview Preparation

100+ Questions. All Answered.

Below are the eight most commonly tested concepts — the questions that separate candidates who built a model from those who truly understand one. Every Excel model in this library contains 6–8 additional Q&As specific to that model type.

Banks · Residual Income
Why can't you use FCFF for a bank?
Because debt is the raw material of a bank's business, not a financing choice. A bank borrows deposits and lends them out — that is the core business model. FCFF requires separating operating from financing cash flows, and for a bank that separation is impossible. The correct approaches are Residual Income (P = BV + PV of excess returns) or DDM (dividends as sustainable distributable earnings).
Infrastructure · Concession
Why is there no terminal value in an airport concession DCF?
Because the concession ends on a fixed date and the asset returns to the government — DIAL in 2036, HIAL in 2068. There is no perpetuity. Every rupee of value must be earned within the concession window. Applying Gordon Growth to a wasting asset would massively overstate value. The model runs for the exact concession term and stops.
Pharma · Pipeline rNPV
What are the clinical trial success rates by phase?
Phase 1 to 2: 65%. Phase 2 to 3: 40%. Phase 3 to Approval: 60%. Approval to Launch: 90%. Cumulative from Phase 1: approximately 14%. Diabetes and obesity runs higher at ~23% cumulative because the mechanism is well-understood. Oncology runs much lower at 5–7%. Interviewers test these numbers directly — know them cold.
Banks · P/BV Formula
Derive the P/BV formula from first principles.
Start from the Residual Income model: P = BV + PV(RI). In steady state with constant ROE and g: P = BV + (ROE–Ke)×BV / (Ke–g). Dividing both sides by BV gives P/BV = (ROE–g)/(Ke–g). JPMorgan at ROE 15%, Ke 10.5%, g 3.5% gives 1.64x. When ROE equals Ke, P/BV = 1 regardless of g — a fundamental result.
SOTP · Conglomerates
Why use SOTP instead of a consolidated DCF for Reliance or Tata Group?
Applying one WACC to TCS at 10.5% and Tata Steel at 12% in the same model simultaneously undervalues TCS and understates steel risk. SOTP values each segment on its own risk profile, peer multiples, and business model. It is always more rigorous than a consolidated DCF for any diversified company. The method selection is itself the answer to a quality of thinking question.
ESG · Climate Scenarios
What are the NGFS scenarios and how do you use them?
NGFS is the Network for Greening the Financial System — 130+ central banks. Three scenarios: Net Zero 2050 (aggressive policy, carbon $250/t by 2050, oil peaks 2025), Delayed Transition (policy delays to 2030s), Current Policies (no new action). For Shell: NZ implies $15/sh, DT $31/sh, CP $53/sh. Probability-weighted at 25/50/25 gives ~$32/sh. Referencing NGFS by name signals institutional-grade ESG integration.
SaaS · ARR Models
Why must you adjust for SBC in a SaaS or platform company DCF?
Non-GAAP EBITDA excludes stock-based compensation — for Salesforce, SBC is $1.3Bn per year, or 13% of revenue. If you value the company on Non-GAAP EBITDA without deducting SBC, you overstate true cash earnings by that full amount. True FCFF must reflect the real economic cost of equity dilution. This is the single most common analyst error on platform and SaaS company valuations.
Marketplaces · Platform DCF
What is take rate and why does it matter more than revenue growth?
Take rate = Revenue / Gross Booking Value. For Airbnb it is 13.5%. Each 1% of take rate expansion on $80Bn+ GBV adds $800Mn of incremental revenue at near-100% gross margin — more than almost any cost-cutting initiative. It is also the primary indicator of pricing power and franchise quality. A declining take rate signals competitive pressure or loss of leverage over hosts and guests.
Common Mistakes

10 Traps That Fail Candidates

The errors below appear repeatedly in interviews and in live deal models. Knowing them is not optional.

⚠️
Using WACC in a DDM
DDM uses Cost of Equity (Ke) only. Dividends flow to equity holders. WACC includes debt cost and is for the firm, not equity holders.
⚠️
Applying FCFF to a bank
Bank debt is raw material, not financing. Use Residual Income or DDM. FCFF requires separating operating from financing — impossible for a bank.
⚠️
Gordon Growth on an airport
Airport concessions are finite. Terminal value = 0. Every cash flow must be earned within the concession term. The asset returns to government at end.
⚠️
One WACC for all segments
TCS at 10.5% and Tata Steel at 12% cannot share a WACC. SOTP with segment-level WACCs is the only correct approach for diversified companies.
⚠️
Ignoring SBC in SaaS
Non-GAAP EBITDA excludes SBC. Deduct it in FCFF. Salesforce SBC = $1.3Bn/yr. Failing to adjust overstates value by billions.
⚠️
Setting g above or near WACC
If g approaches WACC, terminal value approaches infinity. g must be below WACC by at least 200–300bps. Always sanity-check with a sensitivity table.
⚠️
Book value weights in WACC
Always use market value weights for equity and debt. Book value is a historical accident and does not reflect the current opportunity cost of capital.
⚠️
No stranded asset haircut (ESG)
A standard Shell DCF assumes Current Policies forever. Under Net Zero, apply a 30–50% haircut to reserves with more than 15 years of remaining life.
⚠️
Conflating GBV with revenue
Airbnb GBV = $73Bn. Revenue = $9.9Bn. Revenue = GBV × Take Rate. Always confirm gross vs net. Comparing GBV to a hotel's revenue is meaningless.
⚠️
Missing the Netflix content adjustment
GAAP earnings capitalise content spend. True FCFF = NOPAT + content amortisation – cash content spend – capex. Convergence of these two figures is the margin expansion story.
